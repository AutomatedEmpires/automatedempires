## LogLoads

**Keep positioning to software/coordination in development; do not imply freight-broker authority, carrier services, or managed payments.**

### Post 1 — Built for field reality

Timber hauling does not fit neatly into a generic logistics screen. Landings move, road conditions change, equipment matters, and the right operational detail often lives across several calls and notebooks.

LogLoads is being built around that field reality.

### Post 2 — The operating loop

The operating loop we’re designing is straightforward: plan, publish, match, commit, coordinate, haul, confirm, repeat.

The goal is to keep the assignment, route details, trip status, and documents connected.

### Post 3 — Controlled route details

Exact access details should not sit on a public listing.

LogLoads is being designed so general work information can be visible while gate instructions, private-road notes, and precise route details remain controlled until they are needed.

### Post 4 — Different roles, different decisions

A driver, fleet dispatcher, landing operator, and administrator do not need the same dashboard.

We’re building role-specific views around each person’s next decision instead of forcing the entire operation into one generic board.

### Post 5 — Field question

If you work around timber hauling, what creates the most preventable coordination friction today: finding available capacity, matching equipment, sharing access details, tracking trip status, or keeping documents together?

## BidSpace

**Do not imply a live marketplace, bidding, booking, payments, Stripe Connect, or an approved public domain.**

### Post 1 — Invisible commercial space

Some of the most valuable commercial space in a city barely exists online: a market stall, food-truck pad, kiosk, vendor booth, or temporary placement.

BidSpace is being built to make that opportunity easier to see and plan.

### Post 2 — Inventory is specific

One location can contain many distinct pieces of commercial inventory. Booth A12, the north food-truck pad, kiosk 3, and a banner position are not interchangeable.

We’re designing BidSpace around those real-world units.

### Post 3 — Why map-first

A map changes the first question from “Who do I know?” to “What space could fit my business?”

BidSpace is exploring map-first discovery with clearer location, timing, dimensions, and host requirements.

### Post 4 — Flexible allocation

Hosts need flexibility. Some spaces may use a fixed offer, an application, an invitation, or a competitive bid.

We’re designing the structure now; no public bidding, booking, or payment flow is open.

### Post 5 — Early conversations

We’re interested in early conversations with market operators, venues, event teams, municipalities, food-truck operators, makers, and mobile retailers.

What information is hardest to exchange when temporary space is being allocated?
